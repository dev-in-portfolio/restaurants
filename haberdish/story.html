Our Story

Mill town history, Carolina ingredients, and a table built for sharing

What's in a Name?

Haberdish combines haberdashery with dish

We use the table to bring our mill town's history to life through food. With late 1800s and early 1900s migrations from the Appalachian Mountains and rural piedmont of North Carolina, you'll find regional influences in our dishes — bringing touches of that cuisine forward to you. The way we serve our food is on a jumble of plates, offering meats, vegetables, and sides to your table. Think of it like a potluck family gathering with only your favorites.

1800s–1900s
Textile mills like Johnson Mills and Highland Mills define North Charlotte — mill owners build homes for operatives, creating a mill town
2015
Jeff Tonidandel & Jamie Brown open Haberdish in a historic NoDa building — a mill town southern kitchen & craft cocktail bar
2025
Haberdish is recognized by the Michelin Guide as a Selected restaurant
Today
A beloved NoDa staple — serving Carolina-inspired cuisine, craft cocktails from a 1950s soda fountain, and celebrating the stories of Charlotte's mill town heritage

Our Building

A space built from mill town stories

Our soapstone bar top came from a quarry in Virginia. We have salvage denim on our interior seating that was produced in Cone Mills, North Carolina. Our table tops were handcrafted right here in Charlotte, and the wood came from the rafters of our building. The bar taps are reused spindles from a former mill, and our copper lights are hand-spun, designed and assembled right here in Charlotte.

We're lucky to have two sculptures and an in-laid tile piece created by Paul Sires — "the godfather of NoDa." Paul and his wife, artist Ruth Ava Lyons, helped this community transition from the closing of mills to the hip, artistic center it has become.
