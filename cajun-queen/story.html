Our Story

From a 100-year-old Elizabeth house to one of Charlotte's most beloved restaurants

Cajun Queen was born in 1985 when Robert G. saw a 1915 bungalow on East 7th Street and imagined something different for Charlotte—a place where New Orleans jazz met Southern hospitality, and a century-old house became a stage for live music and Creole cuisine.

The House

The Elizabeth neighborhood had been home to this 1915 craftsman bungalow for decades before Cajun Queen arrived. Rather than tear it down, Robert gutted the interior and rebuilt it into three distinct dining levels—each with its own personality, but all connected by the warm, lived-in feel of a real home.

Upstairs, the intimate jazz room. Downstairs, the lively main dining room. Outside, a rooftop patio for warmer evenings. Every room has a view of something interesting, and every seat feels like the best one in the house.

The Kitchen

Executive Chef William Wessling leads a kitchen that honors Louisiana tradition while keeping one eye on what's fresh from local farms and fishermen. The Diane sauce—Cajun Queen's signature—is a recipe that hasn't changed since opening night. The crawfish étouffée uses the same roux technique you'd find in a Baton Rouge kitchen.

The Bourbon Bread Pudding has been called the best in Charlotte by more than a few food writers. The secret, they say, is in the bourbon sauce. We just say it's made with love.

The Music

Every night since 1985, live jazz has filled the second floor. It started with a single piano player on opening night. Now a full Dixieland band performs nightly in a room that feels like a private concert. The band knows the regulars by name, and the regulars know every song by heart.

The music isn't background noise. It's part of the experience—the reason some guests come back every week, the reason first-timers leave as regulars.

The Neighborhood

Elizabeth is one of Charlotte's oldest neighborhoods, and Cajun Queen has been a part of it for nearly four decades. We've watched the city grow up around us—Uptown's skyline visible from our rooftop, new developments rising on every block—but inside these walls, the pace is slower, the music is louder, and the food is always made from scratch.
